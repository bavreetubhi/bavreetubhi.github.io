Regeneratively Cooled Nozzle

Metal additively manufactured regen nozzle for the Solaris MkIII hybrid rocket engine.

Overview

For Solaris MkIII, the combustion chamber and nozzle had to withstand long burn times and high chamber pressures while keeping mass and manufacturing complexity under control. I led the design and CAD of a regeneratively cooled nozzle intended for metal additive manufacturing, replacing a conventional ablative insert with an actively cooled, reusable component.

The nozzle integrates internal cooling channels, structural support ribs and mounting interfaces into a single printed part. The geometry was iterated alongside in-house performance simulations and cooling analysis to keep wall temperatures within material limits while maintaining an efficient expansion contour.

Design Requirements

The regen nozzle design was driven by a combination of propulsion performance, thermal limits and manufacturing constraints from metal additive processes.

  • Operate with the Solaris MkIII hybrid at the target chamber pressure and expansion ratio.
  • Keep wall temperatures within allowable limits for the selected alloy and safety factor.
  • Integrate with the composite combustion chamber and injector mounting pattern.
  • Use channel geometries that can be reliably manufactured by metal 3D printing.
  • Minimise mass without compromising stiffness or fatigue life.

Regenerative Cooling Concept

The nozzle uses a network of axial and circumferential cooling channels wrapped around the throat region, where heat fluxes are highest. Coolant enters upstream, passes through channels close to the hot-gas wall, and exits at the nozzle exit plane, extracting heat while preheating the working fluid.

The channel layout was explored parametrically in CAD, balancing pressure drop, flow distribution and local wall thickness. High curvature regions near the throat were given higher channel density, while the bell section uses longer, smoother channels to limit pressure losses.

Geometry & CAD Implementation

The nozzle contour is based on an axisymmetric bell profile matched to the engine operating point. The CAD model parametrically links key dimensions such as throat radius, expansion ratio and wall thickness, making it straightforward to re-run analyses when the engine configuration changes.

Cooling channels were modelled as swept cuts following guide curves around the contour. Ribs and outer jackets were then built around these channels to provide stiffness and external connection surfaces. Particular attention was paid to transitions at the throat band and at the inlet and outlet manifolds to avoid sharp corners that are difficult to print and inspect.

Thermal & Structural Analysis

Heat flux distributions from engine-level analysis were mapped onto the nozzle inner wall and used as inputs for thermal simulations. The cooling channels were approximated as internal convection boundaries with film coefficients estimated from coolant flow conditions.

Resulting wall temperature fields were then combined with internal pressure loads and external constraints for structural analysis. This checked both local stress levels in the thin throat region and global stiffness for integration with the rest of the engine.

THERMAL MODEL

Temperature predictions along the contour used to verify that peak wall temperatures stay within limits.

STRESS ANALYSIS

Combined pressure and thermal loading checked against yield and fatigue criteria in critical regions.

INTERFACE STIFFNESS

Deflection at mounting interfaces evaluated to keep misalignment with the chamber within tolerance.

Manufacturing Strategy

From the outset the geometry was constrained by metal additive manufacturing rules: minimum channel diameters, overhang limits, powder removal paths and build orientation were all considered while modelling. The nozzle body is designed to be printed as a single piece, with supports located away from critical sealing and flow surfaces.

Internal powder removal is enabled by manifolds and access ports at inlet and outlet plenums. Wall thicknesses and rib spacing were tuned to avoid large unsupported spans and to keep print time and cost reasonable while still taking advantage of the design freedom offered by AM.
